Astrodynamics Space Mission

This astrodynamics course project focused on designing and analyzing an interplanetary mission from Earth to Venus using basic orbital mechanics techniques. Named the “Hell Bound School Bus” mission, the project explored how a spacecraft could safely and efficiently transport passengers between planets while minimizing fuel use and travel time.

The mission began in a low Earth parking orbit and used a Hohmann transfer trajectory to travel toward Venus, which is one of the most energy-efficient methods for interplanetary transfers. The project required calculating and verifying orbital parameters such as position and velocity vectors, eccentricity, inclination, and true anomaly at different phases. The team worked on carefully timing impulse burns to escape Earth’s gravity, transfer between heliocentric orbits, and entering a stable orbit around Venus.

Using MATLAB, the team modeled each phase of the mission, calculated required Δv (delta-v) for every maneuver and the total time of flight. The mission required approximately 149 days to arrive at Venus, with a total velocity change of 23.2 km/s.

This project helped the team apply theoretical astrodynamics principles to real mission design decisions, while taking into consideration the trade-offs between fuel efficiency, trajectory complexity, and mission objectives.
